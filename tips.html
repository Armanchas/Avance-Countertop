Cooking tips

  1. Taste First, Season Second: It may sound obvious, but you should always taste your food before seasoning. If you’re seasoning or marinating meat or poultry before cooking, whisk your marinade or seasoning blend in a separate bowl for safe tasting before adding your protein.
  2. Don’t Pack Your Pan: When it comes to stovetop cooking, haste is waste. Packing your cooking pan with food will keep the heat from distributing evenly, leaving some items raw while others may burn. Pick a pan large enough for the job, and spread your ingredients in a single layer, making sure not to overcrowd the pan. Cooking in smaller batches may seem tedious, but ultimately this will speed up the process. Visit our Pots and Pans guide for more details on the difference between each.
  3. Timing is Subjective: While the timing elements found in most recipes can serve as a helpful guide, be sure to check your dishes by using your own senses. Smell, taste and touch to determine doneness, and use a thermometer for meat, fish and poultry.
  4. Don’t Believe Everything You Bake: Each oven has its own unique hot spots, and the temperature you set the oven to doesn’t necessarily reflect the temperature throughout the oven. To get a better idea of your oven’s true temperature, place a second thermometer in your oven.
  5. Stop Spinning Your Wheels: When roasting or grilling, use 2 skewers instead of 1 to prevent your food from spinning.
  6. Get Even: For an evenly cooked steak, let the meat come to room temperature before seasoning and grilling.
  7. Use the Rule of Thumb: Learn and practice the rule of thumb to check the readiness of steak without cutting into it.
  8. Save Your Starch: Don’t rinse pasta after cooking. The remaining layer of starch helps sauce adhere to the pasta. Rinsing your pasta will cause the sauce to slide, resulting in a bland bite. Just starting your sauce? Reserve a cup of the starchy pasta water to bring the sauce to life. Visit our Pasta guide to learn about different types of pasta.
  9. Use the Wooden Spoon Trick: Before frying anything in oil, test the oil using a wooden spoon. Dip the spoon in and check to see if small bubbles appear. Alternatively, you can throw in a few small ingredients to test the oil, listening for that satisfying sizzle. A low oil temp will cause your ingredients to absorb oil and become soggy, rather than the oil cooking off the food’s moisture for a crispy dish.
  10. Spice Your Food, Not Your Eyes: When cooking with chili peppers, protect your hands and eyes by wearing rubber gloves. Or coat your hands in vegetable oil and wash them with soap and water immediately after handling.
  11. Make it Joyful and Make it Together: Homemade meals are good for the heart and soul. Cook often and cook with others.
  12. Follow Your Nose: Seafood should never smell overwhelmingly fishy; that's a sure sign that it’s starting to go bad.
  13. Prioritize Rest: Let cooked or grilled meat rest at room temperature before serving. This will give the meat time to reabsorb all those delicious juices. Here are a few more grilling tips to become a grill expert.
  14. Taste Test: Always taste your dishes before serving.
  15. Let Your Recipe Be Your Guide: Recipes are only a guideline. Feel free to substitute items that cater to your personal preferences.
  16. Make Your Butter Better: To prevent butter from over-browning in your pan, add a little bit of lemon juice.
  17. Save Your Spices: Keep your spices away from sources of heat like the stove or lights. Herbs and spices can lose their flavor when exposed to humidity and heat.
  18. Keep Your Crumbs: Save old, stale bread to make breadcrumbs in a food processor. You can freeze them for up to 6 months.
  19. Embrace Salt: Don’t be afraid to use salt. This simple seasoning can pull the flavors out of your dishes. Cook with kosher salt and season with sea salt.
  20. Skip the Scoop and Slice: When serving ice cream to large groups, ditch the ice cream scoop. Break open the whole container and slice the ice cream into perfect portions.
  21. Make Your Cheese Grate: If you need to grate soft cheeses, freeze the cheese for 30 minutes for a cleaner slice.
  22. Get the Perfect Poach: When poaching an egg, add a teaspoon of white vinegar to simmering water to help keep the yolk from breaking.
  23. Preheat Your Pan: When sautéing, it’s important to first heat the pan, then heat the oil, then add the ingredients.
  24. Boil Smart, Not Hard: For a great hardboiled egg, bring your pot to a boil and then turn off the heat. Let your eggs sit in the heated pot for 12 minutes and then transfer to cold water. Visit our step-by-step guide for more tips on the perfect hardboiled egg.
  25. Keep it Sunny: Make an ideal sunny-side egg by covering your pan with a lid and letting the steam cook your egg. No flipping required.
  26. Make Your Own Buttermilk: If your recipe calls for buttermilk, you can use regular milk with lemon juice.
  27. Soak Out Starch: For crispy fries or chips, slice the potato, then remove the starch by soaking in water for 1 hour before baking.
  28. Buy the Whole Bird: Purchasing and preparing a whole chicken is more cost-effective than buying specific cuts, and you can use the bones to make broth or stock.
  29. Soften Up: To soften butter, cut slices into a bowl and let sit at room temperature for 10–15 minutes.
  30. Make a Vinaigrette: The basic ratio to make a classic vinaigrette is 3 parts oil to 1 part vinegar.
  31. Practice Cool-down Safety: Help prevent bacteria growth by cooling hot food in a shallow dish.
  32. Stock Up: Make stock in large quantities and freeze in plastic bags for later use.
  33. Make Healthy Swaps: Use Greek yogurt as a healthier substitute for mayo, sour cream, heavy cream and more.
  34. Finish with Oil: Invest in high-quality extra virgin olive oil for special meals or to drizzle over dishes to accent flavors.
  35. Seafood? Eat It: Never over-season your seafood. Accentuate the flavor of the fish with lemon juice, salt and pepper.
  36. Fresh is Best: Look for ground beef that has been freshly ground.
  37. Go Low and Slow: To preserve flavor and prevent burning, it's important to cook slow and keep your heat low.
